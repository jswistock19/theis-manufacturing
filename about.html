JASON THEIS
Chief Engineer & Founder

Inventor of the Theis Tekton composite system. Jason's engineering background spans decades of hands-on construction, materials science, and applied polymer chemistry. He designed the BLT (Bonded Layer Technology) sandwich architecture, developed the TT7200 workhorse formulation, and holds the IP portfolio licensed through HP Miller LLC.

His family's construction legacy dates to landmark California projects of the 1950s and 60s — including Hearst Castle, the Griffith Park Observatory, the Wrigley Mansion on Catalina Island, and a historic Frank Lloyd Wright home. That legacy of building with precision and permanence is encoded into every Theis formulation.

How We Operate

18 Strategic Principles

Theis Manufacturing is guided by 18 core operating principles that govern every decision, every application, and every relationship. Below are six of the foundational principles that define how we operate:

01
American First

Every formulation, hire, and raw material sourced domestically whenever possible. American manufacturing is not just a value — it is our operating model and competitive advantage.

02
Mastery Over Speed

We don't rush quality. Every layer cured right. Every formulation tested before deployment. The Theis standard is mastery, not velocity — because the structures we protect demand it.

03
IP Protection

All formulations are proprietary. Developed and licensed through HP Miller LLC, the IP holding entity of the Theis system. Our formulation portfolio is our most valuable asset and protected accordingly.

04
Vertical Integration

We own the technology stack from R&D through application. No dependencies on third-party formulations, competing IP, or offshore supply chains. The full Theis system is controlled end to end.

05
Prove It First

Every system is field-tested before scaled. No division goes to market without demonstrated, documented performance data. Our track record is proof of concept at every stage.

06
Family Legacy

We build as if our name is on every structure. Because it is. The Theis name has been on California's most important buildings since the 1950s — and that standard of accountability never changes.
